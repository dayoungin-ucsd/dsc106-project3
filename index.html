Research Question

How have thermal anomaly detections changed across U.S. states from 2020 to 2024?

This project explores geographic patterns of thermal anomalies across the United States and highlights regions with consistently high detection activity over time.

Interactive Visualization

Use the year selector to compare thermal anomaly detections across different years. Darker colors represent higher detection counts. Hover over any state to see its thermal anomaly count, annual average land surface temperature, and vegetation index.

Exploring Thermal Anomalies from 2020 - 2024

Shows the average annual surface temperature and average vegatation index for each year

Write-Up

Rationale

For our final design, we decided to narrow our scope to the United States and plot thermal anomalies onto the map in the form of a binned color scale. We chose a binned color scale to color in our map because there were too many thermal anomalies when representing them as points. The interaction techniques we incorporated into this plot includes a dropdown to change year to see how thermal anomalies change over time. The dropdown includes thermal anomalies ranging from 2020-2024. We incorporated tooltips whenever you click on the state which shows how many thermal anomalies were detected for that year. We used 2020 as a baseline and compared the percentage change in thermal anomalies as you switched the year. We also included the percentage change from our 2020 baseline for our vegetation and land surface temperature levels. Some alternatives we considered were adding layers such as vegetation and land surface temperature of states for the year, but we had trouble accessing the data specifically for those layers. Due to this, we decided to move away from this design due to the concern of overplotting. We also decided to scrape through the dataset in order as the dataset was ordered in half-months. Having the data on hand made it so that the output that we were seeing was actually reputable. Additionally, we thought about adding a way to zoom into our states and weren’t sure if this was necessary because zooming in doesn’t add a better experience to the visualization.

Development Process + Division of Work

When we started the development process, we laid out the guidelines for what needed to be completed to ensure we met all requirements. For the checkpoint, we each explored the website by playing around with different layers and experimented on how we could incorporate these different elements into one cohesive question and visualization. After completing the slides together and contributing at least one visualization per group member, Eric recorded and presented the video for submission. He also created the research question that was compelling to all members of the group. For the final visualization, Salina and Dayoung helped with brainstorming ideas while Johny and Eric attended office hours for further insight. Each group member contributed their part in the final code with the following: Salina created the a template for the website with HTML and CSS, Johny converted CSV files to JSON and incorporated the legend with thermal anomalies onto the US map, Eric processed vegetation and land surface temperature data, and Dayoung implemented tool tips for selecting individual states and displaying their data. In terms of the final visualization, Johny made the initial topography visualization that included the total annual of thermal anomalies. Eric, after spending some time writing the python script to scrape the MODIS dataset for the land surface temperature and the vegetation levels, included the average for both into the menu whenever a user hovers over a state. The two were in charge of brainstorming how to effectively incorporate all three aspects. This ensures that users are able to answer the question that our research poses given our visualization. For the final writeup, Johny and Salina wrote and answered the majority of the prompt, while Eric and Dayoung edited along the way. Altogether, the whole process took a combined 40 hours, with the breakdown: Dayoung contributed 10 hours, Johny 10 hours, Salina 9 hours, and Eric spent an estimated 11 hours. The conception of the visualization took the most time, particularly figuring out how we wanted to incorporate the themes we saw from the exploratory graphs into one interactive visualization. From deciphering how to imbue elements that personalize to each viewer, to deciding how the climate aspects can overlay in an easily interpretable way for readers. Most importantly, it was very difficult trying to incorporate the aspects that we had planned to investigate (land surface temperature as well as vegetation levels) to cohesively answer the question of which regions were more susceptible to thermal anomalies.
